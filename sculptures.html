SCULPTURES

Sculpture is thought to be one of the oldest art forms. Sculpting is an indigenous art form in the Philippines that is deeply ingrained in our local cultures. Various carved art forms are part of the daily lives and traditions of ethnolinguistic groups, such as the anito and bulul of the Cordillera, and the okir and pako-rabong designs seen on the sarimanok, naga, and panolong of Mindanao. During the Spanish period, pre-colonial idols were replaced in Christianized communities with images of Christ, the Virgin, and the Santos.

“Mother and Child” 1950

Artist: Napoleon V. Abueva

This adobe sculpture depicts an abstract figure of a lady holding a child who is seated and is being held by her. The young child clings to his mother's head. Napoleon V. Abueva, known as the "Father of Modern Philippine Art," is proud of his Mother and Child sculpture, which is housed at the National Museum.

“Lualhati”

Artist: Guillermo E. Tolentino

The marble picture Lualhati depicts Guillermo Tolentino's child when she was six years old. Lualhati, one of Tolentino's seven children with his wife Paz Raymundo, now calls Germany home. The National Fine Arts Collection only has this sculpture of the artist's relative.

“Sarimanok” 1996

Artist: Abdulmari Asia Imao

The Sarimanok is a decorative bird or a stylized figure resembling a chicken that is well-known in Maranao art. In the Maranao epic "Darangen," it stands for a messenger of datus, or chieftains, and a symbol of monarchy.

“Doxology” 1993

Artist: Julie Lluch

Two life-size figures make up Doxology. One is a self-portrait of the artist lying on the ground, appearing to be in a deep sleep with their hands clasped together, signifying the artist's traumatic experiences and sorrows. The other person is also present, its hands and arms extended, its eyes fixed in prayer on the heavens.

“Harana in Manila” 1955

Artist: Arturo Rogerio Luz

One of national artist Arturo Luz's rarest pieces, and perhaps the only one in this media, is this sculpture. It shows a scene from the Philippine wooing custom known as harana, in which a man expresses his affections to a woman by serenading her.
